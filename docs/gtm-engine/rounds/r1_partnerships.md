## 1. Strategic Framework

### The Core Thesis

Parents trust professionals over advertising. One pediatric dentist who recommends Brush Quest to their patients is worth more than $5,000 in Facebook ads. The hierarchy:

1. **Dentist says "download this"** = 80% conversion. No questions asked.
2. **Another parent says "my kid loves it"** = 40% conversion. Social proof.
3. **School sends it home** = 25% conversion. Institutional trust.
4. **Ad on Instagram** = 2% conversion. Background noise.

The entire partnership strategy works backward from this hierarchy.

### Sequencing Principle

Do NOT pursue all tiers simultaneously. A solo founder has maybe 4 hours per week for partnership work. The sequencing is:

| Phase | Timeline | Focus | Why |
|-------|----------|-------|-----|
| Phase 0-1 | Weeks 1-8 | 10 local dentists + 3 local PTAs | Proof of concept. Learn the pitch. Get testimonials. |
| Phase 1-2 | Months 2-5 | Scale to 50 dentists + school programs + parent communities | Validated playbook. Semi-automated outreach. |
| Phase 2-3 | Months 5-12 | Dental chains + insurance exploratory + brand exploratory | Data and social proof exist. B2B conversations credible. |
| Phase 3-4 | Year 1-3 | Insurance deals + brand partnerships + enterprise | Revenue proves model. Hire Head of Partnerships at $100K MRR. |

### What You Need Before Any Partnership Conversation

Before approaching anyone, you need:
- [x] App live on Google Play (internal testing live as of 2026-04-02)
- [ ] App live in production on Google Play (pending Google review)
- [ ] 5+ genuine Play Store reviews from real parents (not Jim)
- [ ] A one-page "Partner Info Sheet" PDF (see Materials section)
- [ ] A tracking system for referral codes (see Dentist Deep Dive)
- [ ] Landing page with a /partners page (simple, can be a Buttondown form)

## 3. Tier 1: Pediatric Dentists (Deep Dive)

This is THE channel. Everything else is secondary until this is working.

### 3.1 Why Dentists Are the Perfect Channel

- They see the exact parents who struggle with kids' brushing. Every. Single. Day.
- Parents explicitly ask them "how do I get my kid to brush?"
- They have credibility that no ad can match.
- They want to be helpful (it differentiates their practice).
- There are approximately 8,500 pediatric dentists in the US and over 150,000 general dentists who see kids.
- A single pediatric dentist sees 15-25 kid patients per day, 4-5 days per week. That is 60-125 families per week.
- Even at a conservative 10% recommendation rate (only to families who mention brushing struggles), one dentist = 6-12 new families per week = 25-50 per month.

### 3.2 Value Proposition for Dentists

**What is in it for the dentist:**
- Patients remember the dentist who gave them a solution (not just advice)
- Differentiation from every other practice that says "brush twice a day"
- Potential to reduce re-treatment rates (fewer cavities at next visit = happier parents, better outcomes)
- Free to recommend — no cost to the practice
- Makes the dentist look tech-savvy and modern
- Can be framed as "patient education" (which they are already supposed to do)

**What is NOT in it for the dentist (yet):**
- Revenue share — do not offer this at Phase 0-1. It adds complexity and dentists do not care about $0.50/referral. They care about being a good dentist.
- Co-branding on the app — too early to customize.

### 3.3 How to Find and Contact Pediatric Dentists

**Finding them:**

1. **Google Maps search:** "pediatric dentist near Alameda CA" / "children's dentist [city]"
2. **American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry (AAPD) directory:** aapd.org/find — searchable by zip code
3. **Yelp:** Filter for "Pediatric Dentists" — bonus: you can see which ones have good reviews (they care about patient experience and are more likely to adopt tools)
4. **Zocdoc:** Lists pediatric dentists with ratings
5. **Local dental societies:** Alameda County Dental Society, California Dental Association — they have member directories

**Starting local (first 10):**

Search radius: 15 miles from Alameda, CA. This lets Jim visit in person. Here is the outreach plan:

| Week | Action | Target |
|------|--------|--------|
| Week 1 | Identify 20 pediatric dentists within 15 miles | List built |
| Week 1 | Send personalized email to all 20 | 20 emails sent |
| Week 2 | Follow up with non-responders (1 follow-up) | 10-15 follow-ups |
| Week 2-3 | Visit 3-5 practices that responded positively | In-person meetings |
| Week 3-4 | Drop off materials at all 10 that engage | Materials placed |
| Week 4+ | Check in monthly, collect feedback | Ongoing |

**Expected conversion funnel:**
- 20 emails sent → 6-8 replies (30-40% open/reply rate for hyper-local, personalized outreach)
- 6-8 replies → 4-5 interested (most will say "sure, sounds cool")
- 4-5 interested → 3-4 actually place materials and recommend
- 3-4 active dentists x 30 downloads/month = 90-120 downloads/month from first batch

### 3.6 What Materials the Dentist Needs

All of these should be ready BEFORE the first outreach email:

**1. QR Code Download Cards (top priority)**

- Front: "Turn brushing into a game!" + app screenshot + QR code linking to Play Store
- Back: "Recommended by [Practice Name]" (leave blank for generic version, or customize per practice)

- **Can be AI-generated:** Yes. Claude can design the layout. Use Canva for production-ready output.

**2. One-Page Info Sheet (for the dentist, not parents)**

- Link + QR code

- **Can be AI-generated:** Yes. PDF via Canva or even HTML-to-PDF.

**3. Waiting Room Display (optional, for enthusiastic dentists)**
- Small table tent or framed 5x7 card

- QR code
- Only offer this to dentists who actively want to promote it. Do not push.

### 3.7 Referral Tracking System

You need to know which dentists are actually driving downloads. This does not need to be complex.

**Simple approach (Phase 0-1):**

1. Create unique short URLs per dentist: `brushquest.app/go?ref=dr-kim`, `brushquest.app/go?ref=dr-patel`
2. Landing page reads the `ref` parameter and:
   - Stores it in a cookie
   - Passes it as a UTM to the Play Store link: `https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.brushquest.brush_quest&referrer=utm_source%3Ddentist%26utm_campaign%3Ddr-kim`
3. Firebase Analytics picks up the UTM parameters on first app open.
4. Weekly: check Firebase > Acquisition > Campaign report to see installs per dentist.

**What this requires:**
- Landing page modification to handle `/go?ref=` routing (30 minutes of work)
- Firebase Analytics integration (already planned per STRATEGY.md)
- A spreadsheet tracking: dentist name, ref code, cards dropped off, date, installs

**Advanced approach (Phase 2+):**
- In-app referral code entry ("Did someone recommend Brush Quest? Enter their code!")
- Dashboard per dentist showing their referrals
- Only build this when you have 20+ active dentist partners

### 3.8 Making It Effortless for the Dentist

The #1 reason dentist partnerships fail: it requires effort from the dental staff.

**Rules:**
- Never ask the dentist to explain the app. The card does the work.
- Never ask the front desk to track anything.
- Never ask them to hand out cards individually. Leave a stack by the checkout counter.
- Never require a login, portal, or account from the dentist.
- Never follow up more than once per month.
- DO send them a monthly email: "12 families downloaded Brush Quest from your practice this month. Here's what one parent said: [testimonial]." This keeps them engaged with zero effort.

**The dream workflow for the dentist:**
1. Jim drops off cards.
2. Cards sit on the counter.
3. Parents pick them up and scan.
4. Jim replenishes cards every 2-3 months.
5. Dentist gets a monthly "your impact" email and feels good.

### 3.9 Scaling From 10 to 1,000+ Dentists

**Phase 1: 10 local dentists (Weeks 1-8)**
- All in-person. Jim visits each one.
- Learning: What works? What questions do they ask? What objections come up?
- Output: A proven pitch, proven materials, and 3-5 testimonials from dentists.

**Phase 2: 50 dentists in Northern California (Months 2-5)**
- Shift to email-only outreach (no in-person except for enthusiastic ones)
- Use the AAPD directory to build a list of pediatric dentists in the SF Bay Area, Sacramento, Fresno
- Personalize emails using Claude Code: read the practice website, mention something specific
- Mail QR cards directly to practices that respond (USPS cost: roughly $2 per envelope)
- Target: 50 active dentists x 30 downloads/month = 1,500 downloads/month

**Phase 3: 200 dentists nationwide (Months 5-12)**
- Build a "Dental Partner" landing page: `brushquest.app/dentists`
- The page explains the program, shows social proof (testimonials from Phase 1-2 dentists), and has a sign-up form
- Dentists who sign up get a free partner kit mailed to them (cards, info sheet, table tent)
- Promote the page via:
  - Dental Facebook groups ("Dental Nachos" — 30K+ members, "Dental Peeps" — 15K+)
  - LinkedIn posts targeting dental professionals
  - AAPD newsletter sponsorship ($500-1,000)
  - Dental podcast guest spots (e.g., "The Dental Hacks Podcast," "Dental A-Team Podcast")
- Target: 200 active dentists x 30 downloads/month = 6,000 downloads/month

**Phase 4: Dental chains and DSOs (Year 1+)**
- Dental Service Organizations (DSOs) manage 20-200+ locations under one corporate umbrella
- Top targets: Aspen Dental (1000+ locations), Pacific Dental Services (900+), Heartland Dental (1700+), DNTL (100+), Kool Smiles (now rebranded to Benevis, 100+ locations focused on kids)
- These require a different pitch: enterprise-level, outcomes data, compliance review
- Value prop shifts to: "Reduce re-treatment rates. Improve patient satisfaction scores. Differentiate your practices."
- Revenue model at this stage: Dental Practice Plan ($49/month per location for premium access for all patients) or free distribution with data partnership
- Do NOT approach DSOs until you have: 50+ active dentist partners, 5,000+ app users, measurable outcomes data

### 3.10 Dentist Revenue Model by Phase

| Phase | Model | Why |

| 2 | Still free. Dentist gets "Partner" badge on their cards. | Add social proof. "Official Brush Quest Partner Practice." |

## 4. Tier 2: PTAs and School Health Programs

### 4.1 Value Proposition

**What is in it for the school/PTA:**
- Ready-made health education program (saves teachers prep time)
- Engaging activity kids actually enjoy (vs. a worksheet about flossing)
- Measurable participation (classrooms can track completion)
- Aligns with health education standards (most states have dental health in K-3 curriculum)
- Free for schools — zero budget impact

**What is in it for Brush Quest:**
- Bulk distribution: one PTA email reaches 300-600 families
- Institutional endorsement: "Recommended by [School Name]" has weight
- Seasonal repeats: Dental Health Month (February) is an annual program you can run forever
- Network effects: parents talk to other parents at other schools

### 4.2 How to Approach PTAs

**Finding them:**
- Start with your own kids' school. Jim likely knows PTA parents.
- Alameda Unified School District has 9 elementary schools. Each has a PTA.
- PTA websites and Facebook groups are usually public. Find the "health and wellness" chair.
- National PTA (pta.org) has a resource library — getting listed there is a longer-term play.

**Who to contact:**
- PTA Health & Wellness Committee Chair (ideal)
- PTA President (if no wellness chair)
- School nurse (most elementary schools have one)
- Health education teacher (if the school has one)

**Timing is everything:**
- **February = National Children's Dental Health Month (NCDHM)** — this is the golden window. The American Dental Association runs an annual campaign and schools actively look for dental health activities.
- **September = Back to school** — schools set up their wellness programs for the year.
- **January = New Year health resolutions** — secondary window.
- Begin outreach 4-6 weeks before the target month.

### 4.4 "Dental Health Month" Program Template

This is a ready-to-run program Jim can offer to any school:

**Program: "Brush Quest Space Ranger Challenge"**

**Duration:** 14 days (Mon-Sun x 2 weeks)

**Setup (school does this once):**
1. Send parent email (Jim provides the template) with download link
2. Post classroom poster (Jim provides printable PDF)

**During the challenge (families do this at home):**

2. Kids brush morning and evening using the app

**Completion (school does this once):**
1. Jim provides a printable certificate PDF for each participating kid

3. Optional: top 3 brushers per class get a small prize (stickers, pencils — Jim can mail these)

**Why this works at scale:**
- Schools love turnkey programs. This requires exactly 2 actions from the school (send email, hand out certificates).
- The challenge creates urgency (14 days) and social pressure (classmates are doing it).
- Parents see institutional endorsement ("the school recommended this").
- 300 families at a school x 30% participation = 90 downloads from one school.

### 4.5 Classroom Challenge Variant

## 6. Tier 4: Dental Insurance Companies

### 6.1 Why Insurance Is the B2B2C Endgame

The math is simple:
- A child's cavity filling costs an insurance company $150-300 (depending on region and provider)
- A child who brushes properly has 40-60% fewer cavities (ADA data)
- If Brush Quest improves brushing compliance for even 20% of kids, and reduces cavities by 10%, the insurer saves $15-30 per covered child per year
- If the insurer pays $1-2/child/year for Brush Quest access, they still save $13-28/child
- Delta Dental alone covers 80M people. If 20% are families with kids (16M), and 10% of those kids use the app (1.6M), at $1/child/year = $1.6M ARR from a single insurer

This is why B2B2C is the path to $10M+ ARR.

### 6.2 Value Proposition for Insurers

**What is in it for the insurance company:**
- Lower claims costs (preventive care reduces treatment)
- Member engagement metric (insurers are measured on engagement with wellness programs)
- Differentiation: "We offer a free kids' brushing app" is a marketing win for the plan
- Retention: families are less likely to switch plans that provide useful tools
- Data (aggregated, anonymous): population-level brushing compliance insights

**What they will ask for before signing:**
1. Clinical evidence that the app improves brushing behavior (you do not have this yet)
2. Data on user retention and engagement (need 5,000+ users first)
3. HIPAA compliance review (for any health data integration)
4. SOC 2 or similar security certification
5. Integration capabilities (can it connect to their member portal?)
6. A pilot program proposal with measurable outcomes

## 9. Insurance / B2B2C Play — Detailed Roadmap

### 9.1 The Three Stages

**Stage 1: Build the Evidence Base (Months 0-12)**

You cannot sell to insurers without data. This stage is about collecting it.

| Data Point | How to Get It | Target |
|-----------|--------------|--------|
| Daily Active Users | Firebase Analytics | 5,000+ |
| D7 / D30 Retention | Firebase Analytics | >35% / >20% |
| Session Completion Rate | In-app tracking | >85% |
| Brushing Frequency (sessions/week) | In-app tracking | >10 per user |
| Parent Satisfaction | In-app survey or email survey | >4.5/5.0 |
| NPS Score | Quarterly survey | >50 |
| Dentist Endorsements | Partner dentist testimonials | 10+ written, 2+ video |
| Press Coverage | Outreach and organic | 3+ articles in parenting publications |

**Stage 2: Secure a Clinical Validation Partner (Months 6-18)**

The gold standard for insurers is clinical evidence. You do not need a full RCT (randomized controlled trial). You need a credible study that shows the app changes behavior.

**Options:**
1. **Partner with a dental school:** UCSF School of Dentistry, UOP Arthur A. Dugoni School of Dentistry (both in the Bay Area). Propose a pilot study: 100 families use the app for 90 days, measure self-reported brushing frequency and plaque scores at day 0 and day 90.
2. **Partner with a pediatric dental practice:** One of your Phase 1 dentist partners tracks cavity rates for their Brush Quest-referred patients vs. non-users over 6-12 months.
3. **Self-funded survey study:** Survey 500 parents who have used the app for 30+ days. Ask: "Has your child's brushing frequency changed?" Publish the results on your website and in a white paper.

**Cheapest path:** Option 3. A well-designed survey of your own users costs $0 (except time) and produces a citable data point: "82% of parents report their child brushes more consistently after 30 days with Brush Quest."

**Most credible path:** Option 1. A dental school partnership produces a peer-reviewed paper. This takes 12-24 months but is the key that unlocks enterprise sales.

**Stage 3: Run the First Pilot (Months 12-24)**

Armed with data:

1. Target Delta Dental of California (local, innovation-friendly, likely to respond to a Bay Area founder)
2. Propose a 90-day pilot with 500-1,000 member families
3. Pilot terms:
   - Delta promotes Brush Quest to selected families via email
   - Families get free premium access
   - Brush Quest provides weekly engagement reports to Delta
   - At 90 days: measure adoption rate, retention, parent satisfaction, self-reported brushing improvement
4. If pilot succeeds: negotiate a full contract (PMPM or per-engaged-member pricing)
5. Use the Delta pilot results to approach other insurers

### 9.2 Pricing Framework for Insurance

**Rule of thumb:** Your price should be 10-20% of the value you create for the insurer.

```
Value calculation:
- Average pediatric cavity filling cost to insurer: $200
- Average child has 0.8 cavities/year (CDC data)
- Cost per covered child per year: $160 in cavity-related claims
- If Brush Quest reduces cavities by 10% for users: $16 saved per child per year
- Your price: $1.50-3.00 per child per year ($0.13-0.25 PMPM)
- Insurer ROI: 5:1 to 10:1
```

This math is what makes the sale. Insurers buy ROI, not apps.

### 9.3 Contract Structure

```
Pilot Agreement (90 days):
- Fee: $0 (prove the model)
- Insurer provides: member list (email only), co-branded communication
- Brush Quest provides: premium access, onboarding, engagement reports
- Success criteria: >25% adoption, >15% D30 retention, >4.0 parent satisfaction

Full Contract (12-month term):
- Fee: $0.15-0.25 PMPM per covered child (0-12 years)
- Minimum: $50K/year or actual PMPM, whichever is greater
- Includes: premium access for all covered children, quarterly outcomes report
- Data sharing: aggregated engagement metrics (never individual child data)
- Renewal: annual, with price adjustment based on outcomes data
```

- **Cost per partnership-driven download:** Should be under $1 all-in (cards + shipping + time). Compare to paid acquisition CPI of $2-5.

## Appendix A: Objection Handling

**Dentist: "I don't recommend apps to patients."**
Response: "Totally fair. Would you be open to just having the cards available for parents who ask about brushing tips? It's more of a resource than a recommendation."

**Dentist: "Is this safe / does it collect data on kids?"**
Response: "No data collection. The app runs entirely on the phone. No ads, no tracking, no accounts required. It's COPPA compliant. Here's our privacy policy: brushquest.app/privacy-policy."

**Dentist: "What's the catch? How do you make money?"**
Response: "Right now it's free — I'm a dad who built it for my own kids and I'm sharing it. Long-term I plan to offer a premium version with more content, but the core brushing timer will always be free."

**PTA: "We can't promote commercial products."**
Response: "Brush Quest is free, with no ads and no in-app purchases. We're not selling anything — this is a dental health education tool. Think of it like recommending a toothbrush brand, but it's an app."

**PTA: "We need district approval for apps."**
Response: "Understood. This is a home-use app — kids use it with their parents at home, not at school. The school is just sharing the resource, not installing anything on school devices."

**Parent: "I don't want my kid using a screen while brushing."**
Response: "The app is designed to go on the counter, not in the child's hand. They look at it for the mouth guide, but they're holding a toothbrush, not a phone. Most kids stop looking at the screen after the first week — the voice prompts are enough."

## Appendix B: Competitive Moat in Partnerships

**Why can't Colgate just build their own app?**
They tried. Colgate Connect launched and was discontinued. Disney Magic Timer by Oral-B exists but is shallow (a 2-minute timer with AR stickers). Big companies are bad at sustained app engagement because:
- Their incentive is to sell toothbrushes, not build software
- App development is a cost center, not a profit center
- They cannot iterate fast (legal review, brand review, localization)
- They abandon apps when the marketing campaign ends

**Brush Quest's moat:** 10 worlds, 70 monsters, 6 heroes, 6 weapons, voice-guided zone technique, evolution systems, card collecting — this is deeper than anything a CPG brand will build internally. It is cheaper for them to partner than to build.
